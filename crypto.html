A Balanced Guide

How Much Crypto Should You Own?

The honest answer: for most investors, 0–5% of your total portfolio. Crypto is highly volatile, uncorrelated to traditional assets, and has legitimate long-term thesis — but also real risks of permanent loss.

Our framework: Only allocate money to crypto that you could afford to lose entirely without impacting your financial goals. For most people, that's 2–5% of investable assets — enough to benefit from upside without catastrophic downside.

Bitcoin (BTC)

Bitcoin has the strongest institutional adoption, the clearest use case (digital gold / inflation hedge), and the longest track record. Central banks, sovereign wealth funds, and major corporations now hold BTC on their balance sheets.

In 2026, with spot Bitcoin ETFs fully established, you can get BTC exposure through your regular brokerage account without managing private keys or self-custody. Funds like iShares Bitcoin Trust (IBIT) offer direct price exposure with familiar brokerage access.

Ethereum (ETH)

Ethereum is the infrastructure layer for decentralized applications, smart contracts, and tokenized real-world assets. The investment thesis is different from Bitcoin: you're betting on Ethereum's role as the settlement layer of a tokenized economy, not just a store of value.

Spot Ethereum ETFs are also now available, making ETH accessible through standard brokerage accounts.

Real Risks to Understand

  • Volatility. Bitcoin has dropped 70%+ multiple times in its history. If you need the money in 2 years, don't invest in crypto.
  • Regulatory risk. While regulation is maturing, significant policy changes can still move markets dramatically.
  • Concentration risk. Don't let crypto become more than 10% of your portfolio without deliberate, eyes-open risk acceptance.
  • Scams. The crypto space attracts fraud. Never send crypto to someone you haven't verified. Stick to established exchanges.
