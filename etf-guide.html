What Is an ETF?

An Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF) is a basket of securities that trades on a stock exchange like a single stock. When you buy one share of the Vanguard S&P 500 ETF (VOO), you instantly own a tiny piece of all 500 companies in the index — from Apple to Exxon Mobil.

ETFs have three key advantages: diversification (you own hundreds of companies at once), low cost (expense ratios average 0.06–0.20%), and tax efficiency compared to mutual funds.

Key Stat: Over any 15-year period, more than 92% of actively managed US equity funds have underperformed their benchmark index. This is why low-cost index ETFs dominate modern portfolio construction.

Building a Simple Portfolio

For most investors, a three-fund portfolio covers everything:

1

US Stocks — 60%

VTI or VOO gives you broad exposure to the US economy, which drives most of global growth.

2

International Stocks — 30%

VXUS covers developed and emerging markets outside the US, providing geographic diversification.

3

Bonds — 10%

BND reduces portfolio volatility and provides stability during stock market downturns.
